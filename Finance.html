Module 3: Finance Department Training

Objective

This module is specifically designed for the Finance department to equip you with the knowledge and skills needed to recognise and respond to social engineering threats that target financial operations. Given the sensitive nature of the data and transactions handled by this department, it is crucial to be vigilant and proactive in identifying potential threats. This training will cover realistic scenarios, detailed explanations, and role-playing exercises to prepare you for potential attacks.

Scenario 1: Business Email Compromise (BEC)

Imagine you receive an email that appears to be from your CFO. The email requests that you urgently wire a significant sum of money to a new vendor. The message is marked as highly confidential and stresses the need to keep this transaction discreet.

Objective

The objective of this scenario is to help you identify the signs of a Business Email Compromise (BEC) attack. These attacks often involve sophisticated tactics where attackers impersonate senior executives to deceive employees into transferring funds to fraudulent accounts.

How to Recognise a BEC Attack

BEC attacks are often well-crafted and may include:

  • Emails that appear to be from high-level executives, using language and tone consistent with their usual communication style.
  • A sense of urgency, pressuring you to act quickly without following normal procedures.
  • Requests to bypass normal financial controls or approval processes, often justified by the "confidential" nature of the request.
Example: An email from what appears to be your CFO reads: "Please transfer $200,000 to the following account immediately. This is for a confidential project and should not be discussed with anyone else. I need confirmation as soon as it's done. Thanks!"

How to Respond

  • Verify the request: Contact the CFO directly using a different communication method, such as a phone call to their known number, to confirm the legitimacy of the request.
  • Check for inconsistencies: Review the email carefully for any red flags, such as unfamiliar email addresses, slight changes in the email domain, unexpected language, or unusual formatting.
  • Follow company protocols: Never bypass established financial controls or approval processes, regardless of the request's urgency or confidentiality. All requests should be verified through official channels.
  • Report the incident: If you suspect the request may be fraudulent, immediately report the email to your IT/security team for further investigation. Do not take any action until the request has been thoroughly verified.

Scenario 2: Invoice Fraud

A regular vendor with whom your company frequently works sends an invoice for payment. However, this time, the bank account details have changed. The email explains that the change is due to a recent shift in their banking provider and requests that the payment be made to the new account.

Objective

This scenario is designed to train you in detecting and responding to invoice fraud, where scammers attempt to redirect payments by altering the payment details on legitimate invoices. These attacks can lead to significant financial losses if not identified in time.

How to Recognise Invoice Fraud

Signs that an invoice may be fraudulent include:

  • Sudden changes in bank account details or payment instructions, especially if these changes are communicated via email without prior notice.
  • Emails that lack the usual professionalism or consistency you expect from the vendor, such as grammatical errors or informal language.
  • A request for immediate payment, often accompanied by a reason that seems plausible but is unverified (e.g., a "banking error" or "urgent need").
Example: An email from a trusted vendor states: "Please note that our banking details have changed. Going forward, please make all payments to the following account: [New Account Details]. This change is effective immediately due to a transition to a new banking provider."

How to Respond

  • Verify the change: Contact the vendor directly using a previously established, trusted communication method, such as the phone number on file or a known email address, to confirm the change in payment details.
  • Cross-check information: Compare the new bank details with those on file from previous invoices. Look for any discrepancies, and do not rely solely on the information provided in the email.
  • Follow up in writing: If the change is confirmed, request written confirmation from the vendor's finance department, and update your records accordingly.
  • Report suspicious requests: If the change in payment information seems suspicious or unverifiable, report it to your IT/security team before making any updates or payments.

Scenario 3: Payroll Diversion Attempt

An employee sends an email to the payroll department, requesting an urgent update to their direct deposit information. The employee explains that they are facing a personal financial emergency and need the change to be made before the next payroll cycle.

Objective

This scenario focuses on helping you recognise and respond to payroll diversion scams, where attackers impersonate employees to reroute their salaries to fraudulent accounts.

How to Recognise Payroll Diversion Scams

Indicators of a potential payroll diversion scam include:

  • Urgency in the request, especially when it involves changes to payroll information just before a payroll cycle.
  • Unfamiliarity with the email address or subtle changes in the sender's email domain (e.g., an extra letter or a different domain extension).
  • Requests that bypass normal verification procedures, such as changes to direct deposit details without proper documentation or follow-up.
Example: An email from what appears to be an employee reads: "I need to update my direct deposit information ASAP. I’m dealing with a financial emergency, and it’s crucial that the next pay check goes to my new account. It’s crucial that the next pay check goes to my new account. Please make this change immediately, and let me know once it’s done."

How to Respond

  • Verify the request through an alternate method: Contact the employee directly using a phone number or other known communication channel, rather than responding to the email. Ensure that the request is legitimate before making any changes.
  • Check for email inconsistencies: Carefully inspect the email address and domain for any slight alterations or misspellings. Attackers often use email addresses that look similar to legitimate ones but contain minor differences.
  • Follow standard procedures: Ensure that all requests for changes to payroll information are processed according to the company's established protocols, which should include identity verification and proper documentation.
  • Report and escalate: If the request seems suspicious or unverifiable, do not proceed with any changes. Immediately report the incident to your IT/security team for further investigation.

Best Practices for Handling Financial Requests

In addition to the specific responses outlined in the scenarios above, here are some general best practices for handling financial requests and protecting the organisation from social engineering attacks:

  • Always verify: Never take action on financial requests without verifying the legitimacy of the request through an independent communication channel. This is especially important for requests involving large sums of money, changes in payment details, or alterations to payroll information.
  • Follow a multi-step approval process: For significant transactions, ensure that multiple layers of approval are required before funds are transferred. This reduces the likelihood of a single point of failure in case of a compromised account.
  • Educate and train: Regularly participate in training sessions focused on identifying social engineering attacks. Stay informed about the latest tactics used by attackers and share this knowledge with colleagues.
  • Implement strong email security measures: Use email authentication methods like SPF, DKIM, and DMARC to prevent email spoofing. Encourage the use of digital signatures for internal communications to verify the authenticity of requests.
  • Maintain vigilance: Be wary of any communication that urges immediate action, bypasses standard procedures, or involves a high degree of secrecy. Trust your instincts—if something feels off, it’s worth investigating further.

Role-Playing Exercises

To reinforce the concepts covered in this module, we recommend participating in role-playing exercises. These exercises simulate real-life scenarios, allowing you to practice identifying and responding to social engineering threats in a controlled environment.

Exercise 1: Responding to a Suspicious Email

Scenario: You receive an email from what appears to be a vendor, informing you of a change in their bank account details for future payments. The email includes a PDF attachment with the new banking information.

Instructions:

  • Examine the email carefully, checking the sender’s email address, language, and formatting for any red flags.
  • Decide on the appropriate steps to verify the change before taking any action.
  • Simulate contacting the vendor using an alternate method to confirm the request.
  • Role-play reporting the incident to your IT/security team if you identify it as a potential threat.

Exercise 2: Handling a Payroll Diversion Request

Scenario: An employee sends an urgent email requesting that their direct deposit information be changed before the next payroll cycle. The employee claims they cannot provide the usual verification due to an emergency.

Instructions:

  • Assess the email for any signs of phishing, such as a sense of urgency or unfamiliar sender details.
  • Role-play contacting the employee through a secure and verified communication channel to verify the request.
  • Discuss the importance of following established procedures for payroll changes, even in urgent situations.
  • Consider how to report this request to the IT/security team if it seems suspicious.

Exercise 3: Approving a High-Value Transaction

Scenario: You receive an email from a senior executive, requesting the approval of a large wire transfer to a new international supplier. The email emphasises the importance of completing the transfer quickly to secure a time-sensitive deal.

Instructions:

  • Analyse the email for authenticity, paying close attention to the sender’s address, the urgency of the request, and any deviations from standard communication practices.
  • Discuss the steps you would take to verify the transaction, including contacting the executive directly and confirming the details with other stakeholders.
  • Role-play how you would follow the company’s multi-step approval process to ensure the transaction is legitimate and authorised by the appropriate parties.
  • Simulate reporting any concerns or potential red flags to the IT/security team before proceeding with the transfer.
