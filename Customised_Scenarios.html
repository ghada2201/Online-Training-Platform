Module 3: Customised Scenarios for Your Department

Objective

The objective of this section is to provide role-specific training that addresses the unique social engineering threats relevant to each department within the organisation. By understanding these targeted threats and practicing how to respond to them, you will be better prepared to protect sensitive information and prevent potential security breaches.

Finance Department

The Finance department is often targeted by social engineering attacks due to its access to financial data and payment systems. The following content is tailored to help finance professionals recognise and respond to these threats.

Common Social Engineering Tactics

  • Business Email Compromise (BEC): Attackers impersonate company executives or vendors to request unauthorised wire transfers.
  • Invoice Fraud: Scammers send fraudulent invoices or modify legitimate invoices to divert payments to their own accounts.
  • Phishing for Financial Data: Emails or calls that appear to be from trusted sources but are designed to steal financial credentials or sensitive data.

Recognising Phishing Attempts

Phishing emails targeting the Finance department often contain requests for payment, changes in banking details, or login credentials for financial systems. Be cautious of:

  • Urgent requests for wire transfers or payments.
  • Emails claiming to be from trusted vendors with new payment instructions.
  • Messages asking for login credentials or access to financial systems.

Role-Playing Exercise: Responding to Suspicious Requests

Scenario: You receive an email from a vendor you frequently work with, requesting that you update their payment information. The email looks legitimate, but the payment details have changed. How would you respond?

  • Contact the vendor using a known and trusted phone number to verify the request.
  • Check the email address and content for any signs of phishing, such as unusual language, spelling errors, or unexpected urgency.
  • Report the email to your IT/security team for further investigation before making any changes.

HR Department

The HR department is responsible for managing employee information, making it a prime target for social engineering attacks that seek to steal personal data or gain access to sensitive employee records.

Common Social Engineering Tactics

  • Payroll Diversion Scams: Attackers impersonate employees to request changes to direct deposit information.
  • Spear Phishing for Employee Data: Emails that appear to be from trusted sources, requesting sensitive employee information such as Social Security numbers or payroll details.
  • Pretexting: Attackers create a fabricated scenario to convince HR professionals to disclose confidential employee information.

Recognising Phishing Attempts

Phishing attempts targeting HR often involve requests for personal information or changes to payroll details. Be on the lookout for:

  • Emails claiming to be from employees requesting changes to their payroll or personal information.
  • Messages that ask for sensitive employee data, such as Social Security numbers or tax information, without a valid reason.
  • Unsolicited requests for access to employee records or HR systems.

Role-Playing Exercise: Handling Employee Data Requests

Scenario: You receive an email from someone claiming to be an employee, asking to update their direct deposit information. The email looks legitimate, but you don’t recognise the name. How would you respond?

  • Verify the request by contacting the employee directly using the contact information on file.
  • Review the email for any inconsistencies or red flags, such as unfamiliar email addresses or unusual language.
  • Report the email to your IT/security team and wait for confirmation before making any changes to the employee’s information.

Customer Service Department

Customer Service representatives handle a high volume of customer interactions, making them a target for social engineering attacks that seek to obtain customer information or compromise customer accounts.

Common Social Engineering Tactics

  • Vishing (Voice Phishing): Attackers impersonate customers or partners over the phone to gain access to sensitive information.
  • Impersonation: Attackers pose as customers to request changes to account details or access to sensitive information.
  • Phishing for Customer Data: Emails that appear to be from customers, asking for changes to account information or access to personal data.

Recognising Phishing Attempts

Phishing attempts targeting Customer Service often involve requests to change account details or access customer information. Be vigilant for:

  • Calls or emails from individuals claiming to be customers, asking for account updates or access to sensitive data.
  • Requests for customer information that seem unusual or lack proper verification.
  • Messages that ask for changes to customer accounts without proper authentication or justification.

Role-Playing Exercise: Verifying Customer Requests

Scenario: You receive a phone call from someone claiming to be a customer, requesting a change to their account information. The caller is in a hurry and insists that the change must be made immediately. How would you respond?

  • Verify the identity of the caller by asking security questions or using a previously established authentication method.
  • Check the customer’s account history for any unusual activity or discrepancies.
  • Inform the caller that any changes to their account must be verified through proper channels, and report the incident to your IT/security team if you suspect any foul play.
